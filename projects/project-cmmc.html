Supply Chain Security & CMMC Level 2 — GlobalParts Manufacturing

Led CMMC Level 2 compliance program for a 2,500-employee Tier 2 defense supplier with $50M in annual DoD contracts; mapped 6 CUI systems, implemented 14 controls across all NIST 800-171 families, and assessed 3 sub-tier suppliers

Company: GlobalParts Manufacturing
Industry: Defense Manufacturing
Regulation: CMMC 2.0, NIST SP 800-171, DFARS 252.204-7012, ITAR
Contracts: Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, General Dynamics
Date: March 2026

1. Executive Summary

GlobalParts Manufacturing is a 2,500-employee Tier 2 defense supplier headquartered in Detroit, producing precision components for military vehicles and aircraft for major defense contractors. The company processes Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) including technical drawings, specifications, test data, and manufacturing processes for $50M in annual DoD contracts.

Under DFARS 252.204-7012 and CMMC 2.0, GlobalParts must achieve Level 2 certification by implementing all 110 NIST SP 800-171 practices. This project identifies 6 CUI-processing systems, implements 14 CMMC controls across all 14 NIST 800-171 families, assesses 5 CUI protection risks, manages 3 sub-tier supplier security assessments, and prepares SSP documentation for the C3PAO audit.

CUI-R1: CUI Data Spill via Unauthorized Transmission CRITICAL — 20

  • CUI could leave enclave through: regular email, USB drives, personal cloud storage, unencrypted calls, unsecured printouts
  • 600+ people handle CUI daily; human error under deadline pressure creates constant spill risk
  • ITAR-controlled CUI unauthorized disclosure is a criminal offense with fines and imprisonment
  • Treatment: Technical controls preventing unauthorized transmission + making secure path as convenient as insecure path

CUI-R2: CMMC Assessment Failure CRITICAL — 15

  • Current state: 78/110 practices implemented, 22 partial, 10 not started — 32 gaps across AC, AU, CM, IR, SC families
  • Failed assessment = loss of ALL DoD contracts ($50M annual revenue)
  • Treatment: Focused remediation roadmap for 32 gaps; highest-risk areas: System & Communications Protection (6 gaps), Configuration Management (5 gaps)

CUI-R3: Supply Chain Compromise CRITICAL — 16

  • 35+ sub-tier suppliers receive CUI; only 8 have demonstrated NIST 800-171 compliance, 15 have unknown postures
  • DoD holds GlobalParts accountable for supplier CUI protection per DFARS flowdown requirements
  • Treatment: Supplier assessment program, CUI flowdown enforcement, remediation requirements with 12-month deadlines

CUI-R4: Insider Threat CRITICAL — 15

  • 600+ employees with CUI access; threats include espionage, competitor theft, inadvertent disclosure, social engineering
  • No formal insider threat program beyond standard access controls and background checks
  • Treatment: Enhanced personnel screening, continuous monitoring, CUI NDA enforcement, behavioral analytics in SIEM

CUI-R5: Ransomware Disrupting Defense Production CRITICAL — 16

  • Manufacturing was #1 ransomware target in 2023-2024; 2,500 employees create large phishing surface
  • Ransomware on MES halts CNC machines and production — physical production time cannot be recovered
  • DFARS requires reporting cyber incidents affecting CUI to DoD within 72 hours
  • Treatment: CUI enclave isolation, CrowdStrike EDR, application whitelisting, offline backups, quarterly IR exercises

Control 1: CUI Identification, Marking & Handling

  • CUI marking standards per 32 CFR Part 2002: banner marking, category marking (CUI//SP-CTI), dissemination controls, destruction instructions
  • CAD/PLM requires CUI classification before document save; email DLP blocks CUI patterns from non-secure channels
  • Destruction: cross-cut shredding (NIST 800-88) for paper, cryptographic erasure for digital

Control 2: MFA with CAC/PIV for All CUI Access

  • CAC smart card readers on all CUI workstations; PIV-equivalent cards for non-government employees
  • VPN access: smart card + TOTP; shop floor terminals: proximity badge + PIN (for gloved operators)
  • C3PAO will specifically test that MFA cannot be bypassed for any CUI access path

Control 3: CUI Enclave Network Segmentation

  • Palo Alto next-gen firewalls at all enclave entry/exit points with default-deny rules
  • Internal segmentation: engineering, manufacturing, and finance segments cannot communicate directly
  • No CUI system has direct internet access; VPN tunnels use FIPS 140-2 validated encryption
  • Quarterly penetration testing targeting the enclave boundary

Control 4: FIPS 140-2 Encryption for CUI

  • At rest: BitLocker with TPM (AES-256 FIPS validated), database TDE, file-level encryption
  • In transit: TLS 1.2+ with FIPS cipher suites, IPsec VPN with FIPS encryption, secure file transfer with AES-256
  • FIPS 140-2 validation is a hard requirement — non-validated encryption does NOT satisfy NIST 800-171

Control 5: Continuous Monitoring & Audit Logging

  • Splunk SIEM aggregating: authentication, CUI document access/modify/print, network traffic, file transfers, config changes
  • Correlation rules: unauthorized CUI access, bulk downloads, after-hours access, failed auth patterns
  • 24/7 managed SOC with 15-minute SLA; logs retained 3 years on write-once media

Control 6: Vulnerability Management (30-Day Critical Patch)

  • Weekly Qualys scans of CUI enclave; Critical (CVSS 9.0+) patched within 30 days, High within 60 days
  • CrowdStrike EDR + application whitelisting on all CUI endpoints
  • Annual pen test including enclave boundary breach attempts and social engineering

Control 7: Incident Response with 72-Hour DoD Reporting

  • CUI-specific IR plan: detection, containment, mandatory DFARS 72-hour DoD reporting, evidence preservation
  • Quarterly tabletop exercises: ransomware, CUI spill, insider theft, supply chain compromise
  • 72-hour reporting is a non-negotiable DFARS contractual obligation

Control 8: Configuration Management with CUI Baselines

  • CIS-hardened baselines for Windows, Linux, network devices, and applications (Teamcenter, SAP, MES)
  • Least functionality: all unnecessary services, ports, features disabled; application whitelisting
  • SCCM + CrowdStrike monitoring for baseline deviations; all changes require documented approval

Control 9: Personnel Security & CUI Access Authorization

  • Pre-employment background investigation, CUI handling training before access, signed CUI NDA
  • Annual refresher training with assessment; reinvestigation every 5 years
  • Termination: access revoked same day, CUI materials collected, exit interview; for-cause: immediate revocation + 30-day log review

Control 10: Physical Protection of CUI Areas

  • Badge access on all CUI area doors, visitor escort required, CCTV with 90-day retention
  • Secure printing with badge release, GSA-approved security containers, cross-cut shredding with destruction logs
  • Shop floor: CUI work orders returned to secure bins after shifts, photography and personal devices prohibited

Control 11: Media Protection & Removable Media Controls

  • USB ports disabled on all CUI workstations via group policy (hardware-level enforcement)
  • 3 authorized CNC transfer stations per facility: FIPS 140-2 encrypted drives only, mandatory scanning, full audit logging
  • Backup media encrypted with FIPS AES-256 in fire-rated safes; NIST 800-88 sanitization for disposal

Control 12: Security Awareness Training (Manufacturing-Specific)

  • All 2,500 employees: annual general security awareness; 600+ CUI personnel: additional CUI-specific training
  • Shop floor modules: CUI area photography bans, work order handling, USB restrictions, CNC transfer procedures
  • Monthly phishing simulations with manufacturing-specific lures; current click rate: 5.1% (industry avg: 11%)

Control 13: SSP Documentation & POA&M Management

  • SSP describes: CUI enclave scope, all 110 practices with status, architecture diagrams, roles, evidence references
  • 78 practices fully documented, 32 in POA&M with remediation actions, owners, milestone dates
  • POA&M reviewed weekly, reported monthly to CISO and CEO; SSP is the primary C3PAO assessment document

Control 14: Supply Chain CUI Flowdown & Supplier Assessment Partially Effective

  • DFARS 252.204-7012 flowdown in every subcontract involving CUI
  • Annual security questionnaire + on-site reviews for 8 most critical suppliers; SPRS score verification
  • 15/35 suppliers have unknown postures awaiting assessment; suppliers failing within 12 months are replaced

  • Remediation: Config baselines within 6 months, extend log retention to 1 year, first tabletop within 3 months; targeting CMMC Level 2 in 18 months

  • Existing DoD facility clearance at Secret level — security posture exceeds most NIST 800-171 requirements
  • Gaps: 8 IT-specific practices for non-classified CUI systems (config management, system monitoring)
  • Remediation: Close 8 IT gaps within 6 months; lowest risk and closest to CMMC-ready
