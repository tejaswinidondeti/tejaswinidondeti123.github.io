DataPulse Third-Party Breach — Incident Response, Timeline & Root Cause Analysis

Drove the end-to-end incident response, timeline reconstruction, and root cause analysis for NVA-INC-2025-007 — a Severity 1 third-party vendor breach exposing 847,312 customer records across 31,243 enterprise organizations through compromised DataPulse Analytics employee credentials. Closed 158 days after detection with 1.2% customer churn against a 5% worst-case projection.

Company: NovaAI Labs
Incident ID: NVA-INC-2025-007
Severity: 1 — Critical
Frameworks: GDPR Art. 33/34, EU AI Act Art. 73, CCPA/CPRA, NY SHIELD
Duration: 9 Nov 2025 — 15 Apr 2026 (158 days)

1. Executive Summary

On 9 November 2025, the NovaAI Labs security team was notified of unauthorized access to customer analytics data held by DataPulse Analytics, a third-party analytics platform integrated into NovaAI's enterprise product analytics stack. An attacker had compromised a DataPulse employee credential through phishing and exfiltrated nearly two years of accumulated NovaAI telemetry. The breach exposed approximately 847,312 unique customer records across 31,243 enterprise organizations, including customer names, email addresses, organization details, rough geographic location, and OS/browser metadata. No NovaAI production systems were compromised; no passwords, payment data, or chat content were in scope.

This engagement combined three deliverables that together drove the response and the lessons learned cycle:

  • Incident Scenario document — the working incident dossier capturing the breach facts, scope, regulatory exposure, and Severity 1 classification.
  • Incident Response Timeline — the 158-day, five-phase reconstruction from detection through closure on 15 April 2026.
  • Root Cause Analysis report — a 5-Whys investigation, seven contributing factors, and a 12-action corrective programme approved by the AI Governance Committee on 15 March 2026 and endorsed by the Board AI Safety Sub-Committee on 22 March 2026.
Result: Public disclosure delivered on day 18 (27 November 2025) — faster than several industry comparators — while still meeting GDPR Article 33's 72-hour regulator clock. Final customer churn was 1.2%, well below the 5% worst-case projection (~$45M annual revenue at risk).

2.2 What Happened

On 9 November 2025, NovaAI Labs was notified by DataPulse Analytics that unauthorized access had been detected on a DataPulse employee account. Forensic analysis subsequently confirmed the attacker had access for a 72-hour window (6–9 November) during which they exfiltrated NovaAI Labs customer analytics data that had been continuously sent to DataPulse since January 2024. The breach occurred entirely on DataPulse's infrastructure — no NovaAI production system was compromised.

Data Exposed

  • Enterprise customer contact names
  • Email addresses
  • Rough geographic location (city-level, derived from IP)
  • Operating system and browser information
  • Organization names
  • NovaAI API account identifiers (hashed)
  • Usage metadata (session counts, feature usage categories)

Not in scope: No passwords, payment data, chat / conversation content, or model outputs were stored in DataPulse.

2.3 How the Incident Was Detected

Detection source: DataPulse Analytics security monitoring.

At 14:32 UTC on 9 November 2025, DataPulse's security monitoring detected anomalous data export activity from an employee account. The account was suspended within 11 minutes. Forensic analysis determined unauthorized access had occurred over a 72-hour window. DataPulse notified NovaAI Labs at 19:47 UTC on 9 November via their vendor security notification channel. Initial NovaAI triage by the CISO on-call confirmed the data was exported from DataPulse's systems, that no NovaAI production systems were compromised, and that the data exposed was telemetry NovaAI had been sending since January 2024. Emergency incident response was initiated immediately.

2.5 Immediate Context & Prior Connection

Why was data shared with DataPulse? DataPulse Analytics was onboarded in December 2023 as NovaAI's product analytics platform, replacing an in-house tool. The decision was made by the Product team via standard procurement review and DataPulse was selected for its real-time analytics capabilities and SOC 2 Type II certification.

Was this risk known? The November 2025 breach was not anticipated as a specific attack vector. However, broader vendor risk had been identified in the October 2025 Enterprise Risk Register as "medium" for analytics vendors, and the DataPulse relationship had not been subject to enhanced review since onboarding.

Is this ongoing? No. DataPulse confirmed containment on 9 November. NovaAI Labs immediately suspended the DataPulse integration and rotated all credentials on 10 November. No further exfiltration was possible.

Connection to the July 2024 incident: This breach shares DNA with NovaAI Labs' July 2024 plain-text credential storage vulnerability — both reflect insufficient data minimization discipline. Data was being shared or stored that did not need to be there in the first place.

4. Document 3 — Root Cause Analysis Report

The Root Cause Analysis was prepared by the AI Governance Office, the CISO Team, and the Third-Party Risk Office, and was approved by the AI Governance Committee on 15 March 2026 and endorsed by the Board AI Safety Sub-Committee on 22 March 2026. The report uses the 5 Whys method followed by a contributing-factors analysis. Consistent with NovaAI Labs' post-incident culture, the goal is not to assign individual blame but to identify systemic conditions that allowed this failure and to prevent recurrence.

4.1 Five Whys Analysis

Problem statement: NovaAI Labs customer analytics data was exposed when third-party vendor DataPulse Analytics was compromised. 847,000 customer records were exfiltrated.

Why 1 — Why was customer data exposed?

Because NovaAI Labs had been sending customer analytics data to DataPulse for over 22 months, and DataPulse's systems were compromised via a phishing attack on a DataPulse employee, allowing an attacker to access that accumulated data.

Why 2 — Why was so much customer data sitting in DataPulse's systems?

Because NovaAI Labs had configured its analytics pipeline to send broad telemetry to DataPulse — including customer PII that was not strictly necessary for the product analytics use cases. Data minimization was not rigorously applied to vendor data sharing. DataPulse retained this data indefinitely per the default contract terms.

Why 3 — Why was data minimization not applied and why were retention controls not set?

Because NovaAI Labs' third-party vendor onboarding process in 2023 focused primarily on SOC 2 certification status, pricing, and functional capability — not on the question "what is the minimum data this vendor actually needs to perform its function?" There was no mandatory data minimization review gate in vendor procurement; retention terms were accepted as default from vendor templates without scrutiny.

Why 4 — Why was there no data minimization review gate in vendor procurement?

Because the vendor risk management programme assessed vendors against a standardized questionnaire that asked whether appropriate controls existed (Yes/No format) but did not require teams to consider the data sharing design from a minimization-first perspective. Vendors were assessed on their ability to protect data, not on whether the data should be shared at all. The DPO was consulted at onboarding but did not have veto power or mandatory review status for non-financial vendor relationships.

Why 5 — Why did the vendor risk management programme not embed minimization as a first principle?

Because third-party risk management at NovaAI Labs was treated as a security compliance function — "ensure vendors have appropriate controls" — rather than as a privacy and data governance function — "minimize the data we share with any third party so that exposure if they fail is limited." This reflected the organizational structure at the time: TPRM sat under CISO/Security, not as a joint CISO + DPO + Privacy function. The principle that "the data not shared cannot be leaked" was known but not operationalized into vendor controls.

Root Cause: NovaAI Labs treated vendor risk as primarily a vendor-security assurance problem rather than a data-minimization and privacy-by-design problem. The organization trusted that vendors would protect data that did not need to be there in the first place.
Secondary Root Cause: The gap between third-party attestations (SOC 2) and actual operational security at the vendor. DataPulse's SOC 2 Type II certification stated multi-factor authentication was enforced for internal tools, but in practice this control was not operational at the time of the breach.

4.2 Contributing Factors

CF-1 — Over-Sharing of Customer Data with Analytics Vendor

The analytics data sent to DataPulse included customer names, emails, organization details, and location metadata that were not required for the product analytics use cases actually performed. This was convenient telemetry, not necessary telemetry. The principle of data minimization was not applied to the outbound vendor data flow.

Implication: Data minimization must be applied to outbound vendor data flows, not just inbound data collection from customers.

CF-2 — SOC 2 Certification Treated as Sufficient Evidence of Security

DataPulse's SOC 2 Type II report was treated as definitive proof of security posture during onboarding. But SOC 2 reports describe controls at a point in time and depend on the accuracy of management assertions. SOC 2 does not guarantee controls operate effectively on every day thereafter. NovaAI Labs did not conduct independent technical verification of critical controls like MFA enforcement.

Implication: Vendor security evidence should include technical verification for critical controls, not solely reliance on attestations.

CF-3 — No Indefinite Data Retention Gate

DataPulse's standard contract allowed indefinite retention of analytics data. NovaAI Labs accepted these terms. As a result, nearly two years of customer data accumulated in DataPulse systems. Had retention been capped at 90 days, the exposure would have been dramatically smaller.

Implication: Vendor contracts must include specific retention limits proportionate to the business need.

CF-4 — Third-Party Risk Management Under-Resourced

At the time of the incident, NovaAI Labs' TPRM function consisted of 1.5 FTE managing over 340 vendor relationships. Capacity constraints meant vendor reviews were shallow and vendor lifecycle monitoring was minimal. There was no scheduled reassessment cadence for existing vendors after initial onboarding.

Implication: TPRM must be resourced proportionately to the vendor portfolio risk profile.

CF-5 — No Data Flow Mapping for Vendor Relationships

NovaAI Labs did not maintain a comprehensive data flow map showing what customer data was sent to which vendors and for what purpose. When the DataPulse breach occurred, it took nearly 48 hours to confidently confirm exactly what data had been exposed, because the data flow was not documented. This delayed both investigation and customer notification.

Implication: Maintaining data flow maps for vendor relationships is essential for both preventive governance and incident response readiness.

CF-6 — Recurring Pattern: Data Minimization Gap

This incident echoes the July 2024 plain-text credential storage vulnerability, where NovaAI Labs was storing authentication tokens in clear text within operational logs. Both incidents reflect the same underlying issue: data being held (or shared) beyond its functional need, creating exposure that would not exist if data minimization were rigorously practiced. The 2024 incident lessons were not fully operationalized into third-party practices.

Implication: Lessons from one part of the organization must be propagated across functions. The 2024 incident should have triggered a systematic data minimization review across all data stores and flows, including vendor data sharing.

CF-7 — DataPulse Multi-Factor Authentication Gap

At the vendor side, DataPulse had represented in their SOC 2 audit that multi-factor authentication was enforced for all employees with access to customer data. This was not operational at the time of the breach. Their phished employee had single-factor credential access. This is primarily a DataPulse failure, but NovaAI Labs had relied on the assertion without verification.

Implication: Critical control assertions from vendors warrant independent verification for vendors handling significant customer data.

4.4 Lessons Learned

For Third-Party Risk Management

  1. The data we don't share can't be leaked. Minimization is a more robust defense than any vendor control, because it removes the exposure surface entirely. Every vendor data-sharing decision should begin with "what is the minimum we need?" not "what controls does the vendor have?"
  2. Vendor attestations are representations, not guarantees. SOC 2 certification indicates that controls existed at the time of audit per management assertion. It does not indicate controls operate daily. For critical controls on vendors handling significant customer data, technical verification is warranted.
  3. Retention caps should be contractual defaults. Default vendor contracts allow indefinite retention. That is a design choice, not a necessity. Retention should be capped proportionate to functional need, with explicit renewal required.
  4. Third-party risk requires dedicated capacity. Under-resourced TPRM functions default to shallow checklist reviews. The NovaAI Labs TPRM function was handling 340 vendors with 1.5 FTE — a ratio that cannot produce quality reviews regardless of team effort.
  5. Lessons must propagate across functions. The July 2024 credential exposure should have triggered a systematic data minimization review. It did not. Post-incident learning requires explicit propagation mechanisms.
  6. Data flow mapping is an operational necessity, not a compliance artifact. When the breach hit, the team did not know exactly what data had been exposed. That delay cost trust. Data flow maps must be maintained as operational tooling.

For Culture & Governance

  1. Trust incidents compound. This was NovaAI Labs' second major customer trust incident in 18 months. Each on its own is recoverable; recurring incidents erode the foundation. Governance investment must be proportionate to the risk of recurring failures, not just one-off events.
  2. "Responsible AI leader" requires responsible data handling. Responsibility is more than model safety — it includes operational discipline around customer data. The two cannot be separated.
  3. Transparency in disclosure matters. The 18-day disclosure timeline was faster than several industry comparators. Fast, clear, proactive disclosure preserves more trust than slow, defensive disclosure. This lesson should shape future incident response default.
