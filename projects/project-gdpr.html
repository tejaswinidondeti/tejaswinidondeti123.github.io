GDPR Compliance Program — EuroShop GmbH

End-to-end GDPR compliance program for an 800-employee EU e-commerce platform processing 3M+ customer records across 27 member states

Company: EuroShop GmbH (800 employees)
Industry: E-Commerce / Retail
Frameworks: GDPR (EU 2016/679), ISO 27001:2022
Scope: 27 EU Member States, 12 Storefronts

1. Executive Summary

EuroShop GmbH is a mid-size e-commerce company with 800 employees, operating across all 27 EU member states. The company processes personal data for over 3 million customers including names, addresses, payment information, purchase histories, browsing behavior, and marketing preferences. This project implements a comprehensive GDPR compliance program covering 5 data processing assets, 6 privacy risks, 10 controls mapped to GDPR articles and ISO 27001, and 3 core privacy policies.

3. Privacy Risk Assessment — 6 Risks

PR-01: Personal Data Breach Exposing 3M+ Customer Records

CRITICAL — Score: 15 (L:3 x I:5)
  • 3.2M customer records at risk (names, emails, addresses, purchase history, marketing preferences)
  • Attack vectors: credential theft, SQL injection, insider threat, third-party compromise
  • GDPR Art. 33: notify supervisory authority within 72 hours; Art. 34: notify individuals if high risk
  • Financial impact: fines up to 4% annual revenue + compensation claims + reputation damage

PR-02: Unlawful Marketing Communications — Invalid Consent

CRITICAL — Score: 16 (L:4 x I:4)
  • 2.1M subscribers, avg 8 emails/month — invalid consent = millions of individual violations
  • Risks: pre-ticked checkboxes, bundled consent, unclear consent text, missing double opt-in (required in Germany)
  • GDPR Art. 7: must prove when/how consent was given; consent must be freely given, specific, informed
  • Each email without valid consent is a separate GDPR violation

PR-03: DSAR Fulfillment Failure (30-Day Deadline)

HIGH — Score: 12 (L:4 x I:3)
  • GDPR Arts. 15-22: access, rectification, erasure, restriction, portability, objection rights
  • Expected 50-200+ DSARs per month from 3.2M customer base
  • Risks: data scattered across 5 systems, identity verification gaps, no machine-readable export, legal retention conflicts

PR-04: Unlawful International Data Transfer to US Processors

HIGH — Score: 12 (L:3 x I:4)
  • HubSpot (US-based) processes EU customer data; Stripe stores EU data in Dublin but may process fraud data externally
  • EU-US Data Privacy Framework could be invalidated (like Schrems I & II)
  • Risk of forced HubSpot discontinuation — would halt all EU marketing activities
  • Transfer Impact Assessments (TIAs) required per EDPB guidelines

PR-05: Cookie Consent Non-Compliance

HIGH — Score: 12 (L:4 x I:3)
  • 12 localized stores with tracking cookies for analytics, advertising, personalization
  • Risks: missing “Reject All” button (dark pattern), cookies firing before consent, not respecting preferences
  • Country-specific rules: France (CNIL) requires 13-month consent renewal; Germany requires explicit consent for Google Analytics

PR-06: Excessive Data Retention — Storage Limitation Violation

HIGH — Score: 12 (L:4 x I:3)
  • GDPR Art. 5(1)(e): data kept only as long as necessary
  • Risks: inactive accounts (5+ years) with full data, unbounded browsing history, outdated consent records
  • Excessive retention increases breach impact and violates data minimization principle

5. GDPR Control Catalog — 10 Controls

C-01: Consent Management Platform (OneTrust CMP)

GDPR: Art. 6(1)(a), Art. 7, ePrivacy Art. 5(3)  |  ISO 27001: A.5.34

  • Deployed on all 12 storefronts with country-specific configurations
  • “Reject All” button equally prominent as “Accept All” — no dark patterns
  • 4 granular cookie categories: Necessary, Analytics, Marketing, Personalization (all non-essential OFF by default)
  • Consent records logged with timestamp, hashed IP, banner version, and choice
  • Country rules: Germany double opt-in, France 13-month renewal, Italy visible Reject All
  • Google Analytics replaced with self-hosted Matomo in countries where DPAs ruled it unlawful

C-02: DSAR Automated Workflow (OneTrust)

GDPR: Arts. 15-22, Art. 12  |  ISO 27001: A.5.34

  • Requests via privacy.euroshop.eu portal or dpo@euroshop.eu
  • Identity verification: email confirmation + order number or account login
  • Automated queries across all 5 systems to collect all personal data
  • DPO reviews and redacts third-party data; response in JSON (portability) + PDF (access)
  • Acknowledgment within 3 business days; full response within 25 days (5-day buffer before 30-day deadline)
  • Erasure requests: data removed from all systems with deletion confirmation logged

C-03: Data Processing Agreement (DPA) Management

GDPR: Art. 28, Arts. 44-49  |  ISO 27001: A.5.20

  • DPAs with all 5 processors: HubSpot (SCCs + supplementary measures), Stripe (EU data residency), Personio (EU-only), AWS (EU-West-1), OneTrust (EU-only)
  • Each DPA includes: processing purposes/data types, sub-processor approval rules, 24-hour breach notification, audit rights, data return/deletion at contract end
  • Annual review cycle; updated when processing activities change

C-04: Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) Process

GDPR: Art. 35, Art. 36  |  ISO 27001: A.5.34

  • Triggered by: sensitive data processing, large-scale monitoring, automated decisions, new international transfers, major processing changes
  • Assesses: data processed, necessity, risks to individuals, risk mitigation measures, DPO approval
  • Completed DPIAs: e-commerce tracking, HubSpot marketing profiling, Stripe fraud detection, employee health data (Personio)
  • High residual risk requires prior consultation with supervisory authority (Art. 36)

C-05: 72-Hour Breach Notification Procedure

GDPR: Art. 33, Art. 34  |  ISO 27001: A.5.24, A.5.26

  • 0-4 hours: security team confirms breach and PHI involvement
  • 4-12 hours: DPO assesses risk to individuals using EDPB guidelines
  • 12-48 hours: DPO prepares notification to German BfDI (lead supervisory authority)
  • 48-72 hours: notification submitted; high-risk individuals notified in their language
  • Monthly drills to validate 72-hour response capability

C-06: Data Encryption at Rest and in Transit

GDPR: Art. 32, Art. 5(1)(f)  |  ISO 27001: A.8.24

  • At rest: AES-256 via AWS KMS customer-managed keys (databases, S3, backups)
  • In transit: TLS 1.3 for all connections (APIs, internal services, third-party integrations)
  • Encrypted data breach may qualify for Art. 34 exemption (no individual notification required)

C-07: Role-Based Access Control with Quarterly Reviews

GDPR: Art. 32, Art. 5(1)(f)  |  ISO 27001: A.5.15, A.8.5

  • Purpose-based roles: Customer Service (names/emails/orders), Marketing (emails + anonymized segments), Data Engineering (full DB), Finance (payment data), HR (employee data), DPO (read-only audit)
  • MFA required for all roles; quarterly access reviews
  • Terminated employees lose access within 1 hour via Okta

C-08: Automated Data Retention & Deletion Enforcement

GDPR: Art. 5(1)(e), Art. 17  |  ISO 27001: A.5.33

  • Retention periods: active accounts + 2 years, purchases 7 years, browsing data anonymized after 90 days, marketing consent active + 3 years, payment info 90 days after last transaction
  • Daily automated jobs flag and anonymize/delete expired data using k-anonymity
  • DSAR erasure requests fulfilled within 30 days with DPO approval

C-09: International Data Transfer Safeguards

GDPR: Arts. 44-49  |  ISO 27001: A.5.34

  • HubSpot (US): Standard Contractual Clauses + encryption + Transfer Impact Assessment
  • Google Analytics replaced with self-hosted Matomo (EU-only) after DPA rulings
  • Annual TIA review; DPO maintains register of all international transfers with legal basis and safeguards

C-10: Privacy-by-Design & Privacy-by-Default in Development

GDPR: Art. 25  |  ISO 27001: A.8.25

  • Privacy screening checklist for all new features
  • Privacy defaults: marketing OFF, data sharing minimized, only necessary data collected
  • Pseudonymization when full identifiers not needed; automated code scanning for personal data
  • Annual privacy-by-design training for all developers

6. Core Privacy Policies — 3 Policies

Policy 1: Customer Privacy Policy (External)

OwnerDPO
StatusApproved
  • Published on all 12 storefronts in local languages
  • Covers: controller identity, DPO contact, data categories & purposes, recipients (HubSpot, Stripe, AWS), international transfers & safeguards, retention periods, all data subject rights, automated decision-making
  • Plain language with summary + full details; version-tracked; reviewed annually

Policy 2: DSAR Response Policy (Internal)

OwnerDPO
StatusApproved
  • Channels: portal, email, mail, in-person; identity verification required
  • Request types: access, correction, deletion, restriction, portability, objection, automated decisions
  • SLAs: acknowledgment within 3 business days, full response within 25 days
  • Escalation for complex requests (2-month extension); refusal criteria for unfounded/excessive requests
  • Annual metrics reported to management

Policy 3: Employee Data Handling & Classification Policy

OwnerDPO + Head of HR
StatusApproved
  • Covers: data classification, handling procedures, clean desk/screen, encrypted email, approved purposes only
  • No storing personal data on personal devices/cloud; need-to-know sharing only
  • GDPR training within 30 days of joining + annual refreshers; extra training for special category data handlers
  • Available in all 8 office languages; consequences for violations documented
