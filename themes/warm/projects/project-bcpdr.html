Business Continuity & Disaster Recovery — CriticalOps Inc

Led BCP/DR planning for a critical infrastructure operator managing SCADA systems across 12 water treatment plants and 28 power grid substations serving 10M+ citizens; defined RTO/RPO for 5 critical assets, assessed 4 continuity risks, and implemented 8 business continuity controls

Company: CriticalOps Inc
Industry: Critical Infrastructure (Water & Energy)
Frameworks: CISA, NERC CIP, EPA AWIA, COBIT, ISO 27001
Scale: 900 Employees • 12 Water Plants • 28 Substations • 10M+ Citizens
Date: March 2026

1. Executive Summary

CriticalOps Inc is a 900-employee critical infrastructure operator managing SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition) systems for water treatment facilities and power grid substations serving over 10 million citizens across 3 states. Classified as critical infrastructure under CISA guidelines and subject to NERC CIP standards, CriticalOps must maintain operational resilience capable of withstanding cyberattacks, natural disasters, equipment failures, and supply chain disruptions.

This project conducts a Business Impact Analysis (BIA), identifies 5 critical assets with defined RTO/RPO requirements, assesses 4 continuity risks, and documents 8 business continuity controls mapped to ISO 27001 and COBIT frameworks.

Risk 1: Cyberattack on SCADA Systems CRITICAL — 20

CategoryAvailability
StatusOpen
OwnerOT Security Manager + Business Continuity Manager

Nation-state actors and cybercriminal groups actively target water and energy SCADA systems. Attack vectors include spear-phishing of employees with OT access, exploitation of the IT/OT boundary, supply chain attacks through compromised vendor software updates, insider threats, and exploitation of legacy industrial protocols (Modbus, DNP3) that lack authentication. A successful attack could alter chemical dosing in water treatment (public health emergency), trip circuit breakers causing widespread power outages, or damage physical equipment through manipulation of control parameters.

  • Likelihood 4 (Likely): CISA reports critical infrastructure targeting increased 300% since 2020; Russian-linked groups (Sandworm, Volt Typhoon) specifically target water and energy systems
  • Impact 5 (Severe): Compromised water treatment could poison a city’s supply affecting millions; this is a public safety and national security risk measured in human lives

Risk 2: Natural Disaster Destroying Primary Control Center CRITICAL — 15

CategoryPhysical Security
StatusOpen
OwnerBusiness Continuity Manager

The primary control center could be destroyed or rendered inoperable by earthquake, hurricane, fire, or prolonged regional power outage. Loss means loss of centralized SCADA monitoring for all 12 water plants and 28 substations, loss of the primary Historian database, and potential loss of communication infrastructure if fiber routes are damaged.

  • Likelihood 3 (Possible): Climate change is increasing frequency and severity of extreme weather; the Texas Winter Storm, Hurricane Katrina, and 2011 Joplin tornado all destroyed critical infrastructure facilities
  • Impact 5 (Severe): Complete loss of centralized control over water and power serving 10M citizens; this risk is why the EOC/DR site exists 150 miles away

Risk 3: Key Personnel Unavailability During Extended Emergency CRITICAL — 16

CategoryHuman Resources
StatusOpen
OwnerHR Director + Business Continuity Manager

CriticalOps requires specialized SCADA operators, control system engineers, and OT security specialists who are extremely difficult to replace. Only 18 operators are certified for water SCADA and 12 for energy EMS. Single points of failure exist for 3 critical roles: OT Security Manager, Lead SCADA Engineer, and Historian Database Administrator. No formal cross-training program currently exists for these specialized roles.

  • Likelihood 4 (Likely): COVID-19 proved 30–40% workforce unavailability is not theoretical; severe weather events simultaneously affect employees’ personal lives
  • Impact 4 (Major): Operations can continue with reduced staff using manual procedures, but decision-making quality degrades under fatigue and stress

Risk 4: Supply Chain Disruption Affecting SCADA Replacement Parts HIGH — 12

CategoryVendor Risk
StatusOpen
OwnerProcurement Director + OT Security Manager

SCADA systems rely on specialized hardware (PLCs, RTUs, HMIs) from a small number of manufacturers (Siemens, Schneider Electric, ABB, Rockwell Automation). Currently: only 2-week spare parts inventory for critical PLCs and RTUs, no alternative vendor qualified for the primary SCADA platform, and 3 critical components are single-sourced with 9+ month lead times.

  • Likelihood 3 (Possible): Global chip shortage of 2021–2023 proved supply chain disruption is real; industrial component lead times remain elevated
  • Impact 4 (Major): Without replacement parts, failed equipment forces degraded manual mode or shutdown; 2-week inventory insufficient for extended events

Control 1: Redundant SCADA Architecture with Automatic Failover Effective

FrameworkISO 27001 A.8.14 • COBIT APO09 • NIST CSF PR.PT-5
FrequencyContinuous (automatic failover in 30 seconds)
OwnerDirector of Water Operations + Director of Grid Operations

All SCADA and EMS systems operate in active/hot-standby configuration with automatic failover in 30 seconds. Field devices at all 12 water plants and 28 substations are configured with dual communication paths to both primary and standby servers. Failover tested monthly with planned switchovers.

  • Water SCADA: primary in Control Center A, hot standby in Control Center B (same campus, different building, separate power feed)
  • Energy EMS: primary in Grid Control Center, hot standby in the EOC/DR site
  • RTO achieved: 30 seconds (well within 15-minute water and 5-minute energy requirements)

Control 2: Disaster Recovery Site with Full Operational Capability Effective

FrameworkISO 27001 A.5.30 • COBIT DSS04 • NIST CSF RC.RP-1
FrequencyQuarterly (full failover exercise)
OwnerBusiness Continuity Manager

The EOC/DR site provides complete redundancy with quarterly full failover exercises where all operations are transferred, the primary site is simulated as destroyed, and operations run from the EOC for 4+ hours.

  • Most Recent Exercise Results: Water SCADA failover in 12 minutes (target 15), Energy EMS failover in 4 minutes (target 5), Historian available in 3.5 hours (target 4)
  • All targets met with documented lessons learned and corrective actions

Control 3: OT/IT Network Segmentation with Air Gap & Data Diodes Effective

FrameworkISO 27001 A.8.22 • COBIT APO13 • NERC CIP-005 • NIST CSF PR.AC-5
FrequencyContinuous (enforced by physical architecture)
OwnerOT Security Manager

The OT network is physically separated from IT and internet through 5 enforcement layers:

  • Layer 1 – Physical: OT equipment in secured rooms with badge + biometric access; no Wi-Fi in OT zones
  • Layer 2 – Air Gap: No routable network path between OT and the internet
  • Layer 3 – Data Diodes: Hardware-enforced one-way data flow (OT→IT only) for Historian replication
  • Layer 4 – Industrial Firewalls: Palo Alto with ICS protocol inspection at IT/OT boundary
  • Layer 5 – Protocol Isolation: OT uses Modbus, DNP3, IEC 61850 (not routable on IT network)
  • Air gap verified quarterly through penetration testing — no successful crossing achieved

Control 4: Manual Override Procedures for All Critical Operations Effective

FrameworkCOBIT DSS04.04 • ISO 27001 A.5.30 • NIST CSF RC.RP-1
FrequencyMonthly (manual operation drills at each facility)
OwnerDirector of Water Operations + Director of Grid Operations

Every water treatment plant and power substation maintains documented manual override procedures for operation without SCADA.

  • Water Plants: Manual chemical dosing, manual pump operation via local control panels, manual water quality instrument readings
  • Substations: Manual breaker operation, manual voltage regulation, radio-coordinated load management
  • Monthly 2-hour drills with SCADA deliberately disconnected; annual 8-hour extended manual operation exercise
  • Cross-facility coordination uses dedicated radio network independent of IT/OT networks

Control 5: Critical Spare Parts Inventory & Vendor Diversification Partially Effective

FrameworkCOBIT APO10 • ISO 27001 A.5.21 • NIST CSF ID.SC-2
FrequencyMonthly (inventory review) + Annual (vendor assessment)
OwnerProcurement Director

Maintains on-site inventory of critical SCADA components: PLCs (8 spares), RTUs (12), HMI workstations (6), industrial switches (10), serial-to-IP converters (20), and power supplies (15). Inventory levels cover simultaneous failure of 3 critical components plus 1 buffer.

  • Gap: Current 2-week inventory is insufficient for extended events
  • Gap: Secondary vendor qualification is in evaluation phase (2 candidates) but not yet complete
  • Gap: 3 single-source components with 9+ month lead times remain unaddressed
  • Emergency procurement agreements with primary vendors guarantee 48-hour delivery during declared emergencies

Control 6: Workforce Continuity & Cross-Training Program Partially Effective

FrameworkISO 27001 A.6.3 • COBIT APO07 • NIST CSF PR.AT-1
FrequencyQuarterly (cross-training) + Annual (succession planning)
OwnerHR Director + Operations Directors

Addresses key personnel risk through cross-training, succession planning, pandemic preparedness, and mutual aid agreements.

  • Cross-Training: Each critical SCADA role targets minimum 2 backup-qualified operators — currently 60% of roles have 2+ backups
  • Succession Planning: Successors identified for 3 single-point-of-failure roles but not yet fully trained
  • Pandemic Preparedness: Remote SCADA monitoring from approved home locations (secured VPN, monitoring only)
  • Gap: Mutual aid agreements signed with only 2 of 5 target neighboring utilities

Control 7: Cyber Incident Response Plan for ICS/SCADA Effective

FrameworkISO 27001 A.5.24–5.28 • COBIT DSS02 • NIST CSF RS.RP-1 • CISA ICS-CERT
FrequencyQuarterly (tabletop) + Annual (full-scale simulation)
OwnerOT Security Manager

ICS-specific incident response plan prioritizing operational safety over standard IT security practices.

  • Detection: OT-specific monitoring tools (Claroty/Dragos for ICS network monitoring, not standard IT SIEM)
  • Containment: Prioritizes operational safety — SCADA systems controlling active water treatment are not immediately isolated without ensuring safe manual operation first
  • Reporting: CISA notification within 72 hours per CIRCIA; coordination with FBI, CISA ICS-CERT, EPA Water ISAC, DOE E-ISAC
  • Recovery: PLC firmware integrity verification required before returning to automated SCADA control
  • Exercises: Quarterly tabletops rotating 4 scenarios (ransomware, insider threat, supply chain compromise, DR activation); annual full-scale simulation with CISA participation

Control 8: Business Impact Analysis & RTO/RPO Governance Effective

FrameworkCOBIT DSS04.02 • ISO 27001 A.5.30 • NIST CSF ID.BE-5
FrequencyAnnual (full BIA review) + triggered by significant changes
OwnerBusiness Continuity Manager

Formal BIA process documenting all critical business functions ranked by impact on public safety, regulatory compliance, financial loss, and reputational damage. BIA is approved by CEO and Board of Directors and shared with regulators upon request.

  • BIA drives: DR investment priorities, testing frequency, spare parts inventory levels, and staffing requirements
  • Updated annually and triggered when: new facilities added, significant technology changes, regulatory changes, or real incidents reveal gaps
