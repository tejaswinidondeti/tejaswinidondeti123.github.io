AI Governance Program Build

NovaAI Labs — Responsible AI Policy, Operating Model & Use Case Review Process

Authored a complete frontier AI governance program for NovaAI Labs — a Responsible AI Policy with 8 principles, an Operating Model defining 8 governance bodies and a 9-decision authority matrix, and an end-to-end AI Use Case Review process with 6 stages and 4 risk-tier review pathways from 2 weeks (standard) to 12 weeks (frontier).

Company: NovaAI Labs
Industry: Frontier AI / Foundation Models
Frameworks: EU AI Act (Articles 51–53, 71), GDPR, NIST AI RMF, EU AI Pact, Bletchley Declaration
Scope: 4 Geographies • All Frontier & Applied AI Systems
Date: April 2026

1. Executive Summary

This engagement delivered a complete, board-ready responsible AI program for NovaAI Labs — a frontier AI provider operating across the United States, European Union, United Kingdom, and Japan. The program is built around three integrated documents that together form the operational foundation of the company's AI governance:

  • Responsible AI Policy (v2.0) — the principles, scope, and lifecycle requirements that govern every AI system NovaAI develops or deploys.
  • AI Governance Operating Model (v2.0) — the committees, roles, decision authorities, and reporting calendar that operationalize the policy.
  • AI Use Case Review & Approval Process (v2.0) — the end-to-end intake-to-monitoring workflow that brings every AI system through a proportionate review pathway.

All three documents were issued in April 2026 as part of a renewed responsible-AI commitment following 2025 safety-team concerns, organisational restructuring, the November 2025 DataPulse vendor breach, and the February 2026 NovaHire bias findings. The program reflects NovaAI's dual role as both an AI provider (delivering enterprise solutions) and an AI deployer (running its own internal AI tools), and is aligned with the EU AI Act high-risk and GPAI-systemic-risk regimes that came into force in January 2026.

Program Headline: 8 principles • 8 governance bodies • 9-decision authority matrix • 4 review pathways (2 to 12 weeks) • 6 lifecycle stages • quarterly + annual reporting cadence • current maturity Defined, 12-month target Optimised.

2. Document 1 — Responsible AI Policy (v2.0)

The Responsible AI Policy is co-owned by the Chief AI Officer and Chief Risk Officer and applies to all employees, contractors, research affiliates, and third parties engaged in AI development or deployment. It is classified Internal — All Employees and is on a minimum annual review cycle, with the next review scheduled for October 2026.

2.1 Purpose & Dual Role

NovaAI Labs operates in two critical capacities — as an AI provider delivering enterprise-grade AI solutions to customers, and as a deployer of its own internally-developed AI tools across research, operations, and product teams. This dual role requires rigorous governance at every stage of the AI lifecycle. The policy explicitly positions itself as a renewal of NovaAI's responsible-AI commitment, informed by the 2024–2026 incidents and organisational learnings, and aimed at rebuilding stakeholder trust through enhanced transparency, stronger safety practices, and systematic accountability.

2.3 The Eight Core Principles

3.1 — Safety-First Development

Pre-training risk assessments for all frontier models; dangerous capability evaluations before release; staged deployment with continuous safety monitoring; mandatory red-teaming; systematic dual-use risk evaluation. Strengthened following 2025 safety-team concerns; all frontier models must pass safety review before deployment authorisation.

3.2 — Fairness and Non-Discrimination

No unlawful discrimination on protected characteristics in any NovaAI system. Mandatory bias audits for all consequential AI applications. The February 2026 NovaHire bias findings have informed systematic improvements to fairness evaluation and remediation. Debiased training data practices and continuous fairness monitoring are required.

3.3 — Transparency

Model cards published for all deployed models and system cards for applied products. AI use is disclosed to end users. Quarterly transparency reports are published. Internal AI System Inventory is maintained for governance purposes.

3.4 — Accountability

Every AI system has a named system owner responsible for governance compliance. Escalation paths are documented. Board-level oversight integrates AI strategy and risk management with corporate governance. The Chief AI Officer holds operational accountability for AI safety.

3.5 — Privacy and Data Protection

Minimised data collection and consent-based data practices in training data sourcing. Customer data protected throughout the model lifecycle. Informed by the November 2025 DataPulse vendor breach, vendor data access is now minimised through contractual controls. Informed by the July 2024 credential exposure vulnerability, encryption at rest is mandatory for all sensitive data.

3.6 — Human Oversight

Meaningful human review is required for all consequential decisions. Rubber-stamp oversight is unacceptable and will be treated as a governance violation. Override criteria are documented; oversight personnel receive mandatory training on responsible AI assessment.

3.7 — Security and Robustness

Adversarial testing before deployment; continuous security monitoring; incident response capabilities maintained. Enhanced third-party vendor security requirements implemented post-DataPulse incident to ensure supply-chain security.

3.8 — Societal Impact and Alignment

Investment in alignment research to reduce existential risks; engagement with policymakers, researchers, and civil society. NovaAI honours voluntary commitments including the EU AI Pact, the White House Voluntary Commitments, and the Bletchley Declaration on AI Safety.

2.5 Vendor & Third-Party AI

Enhanced due diligence has been implemented following the November 2025 DataPulse vendor breach. Vendor data access is minimised through contractual controls and technical restrictions. All vendor contracts include AI Act compliance requirements applicable to their jurisdiction. Vendors are granted security audit rights, and an annual vendor review cycle is mandatory. Vendors deploying AI systems on NovaAI infrastructure must comply with this policy in full.

2.6 Training, Culture & Whistleblower Protection

The policy establishes four mandatory training programmes:

  • AI Governance Fundamentals — all staff, annual
  • Frontier Model Safety — all research staff
  • Responsible Deployment — all product team members
  • Human Oversight and Decision-Making — designated oversight personnel

Whistleblower protections are explicitly reaffirmed for safety concerns. In response to the 2025 safety-team departures, the policy strengthens protections for employees raising responsible-AI concerns, including confidential reporting channels and non-retaliation guarantees. Policy breaches are escalated to the Chief Compliance Officer; sanctions range from formal warning to termination.

2.7 References & External Commitments

The policy is anchored to: the EU AI Act (Articles 51–53 GPAI obligations), the UK AI Bill framework, Japanese AI governance guidelines, and the US Executive Order on AI governance. Voluntary commitments honoured: EU AI Pact & Code of Practice, White House Voluntary AI Commitments, and the Bletchley Declaration on AI Safety. Internal references include the AI Governance Framework (2025), Data Security and Privacy Policy, Vendor Risk Management Standard, NovaHire Bias Audit Report (Feb 2026), and DataPulse Incident Review (Nov 2025).

3. Document 2 — AI Governance Operating Model (v2.0)

The Operating Model translates the policy into a working machine: it defines who sits on which committee, what each role is accountable for, how decisions flow, and how AI governance integrates with the company's existing enterprise risk, security, legal, and data protection functions.

3.1 Why an Operating Model

Effective AI governance is not about policies on paper — it happens in real decisions, conversations, and processes. This operating model ensures that frontier AI research and product decisions are made with adequate safety oversight, cross-functional input, and clear accountability. It reflects a strengthened commitment to independent safety oversight at the Board level following the 2025 safety incidents and restructuring.

3.3 Committee & Role Descriptions

Board of Directors

Holds ultimate governance responsibility for AI strategy and safety. Approves the annual AI governance framework, receives the annual comprehensive AI Governance Programme Report, and is informed of all material AI incidents and escalations.

Board AI Safety Sub-Committee New — Jan 2026

Structure: Chaired by an Independent Director with AI safety expertise; three independent directors plus the CEO as a non-voting observer; direct reporting line to the full Board.

Responsibilities: Reviews frontier model release decisions, can veto frontier model releases, receives quarterly safety reports, and provides independent oversight of NovaAI's AI safety commitments.

Cadence: Quarterly + extraordinary sessions.

AI Governance Committee

Mandate: Operational oversight of AI governance.

Members: CRO (Chair), CAIO, CTO, DPO, General Counsel, CISO, Head of AI Safety (non-voting), CFO (observer).

Responsibilities: Approves high-risk and frontier AI system deployments; reviews the quarterly AI Governance Dashboard; owns and maintains the Responsible AI Policy; reviews material incidents; resolves governance disputes.

Cadence: Quarterly + extraordinary sessions.

AI Safety Review Board

Mandate: Frontier model and novel-capability safety decisions.

Members: Head of AI Safety (Chair), three rotating senior research scientists, an external safety advisor, a Chief AI Officer representative, and the Red Team Lead.

Responsibilities: Approves frontier training runs above the compute threshold, reviews dangerous capability evaluation results, gates frontier deployments, reviews compliance with the responsible scaling policy, and can halt training or deployment.

Cadence: Monthly + on-demand for frontier decisions.

AI Governance Office — 4 FTE

Head: AI Governance Lead • Team: Policy & Compliance Analyst, Risk Assessment Analyst, Programme Coordinator. Dual-reports to the CRO and CAIO.

Responsibilities: Maintains the AI System Inventory; receives, triages and coordinates all AUCRs; produces standard risk assessments; coordinates enhanced review panels; monitors the regulatory landscape; delivers AI governance training; maintains the AI incident log; and produces both the quarterly AI Governance Dashboard and the annual AI Governance Programme Report.

Functional AI Champions

One per business unit, serving as embedded governance contacts. Coverage spans ML Research / Frontier Product, Enterprise Products (NovaChat Enterprise), NovaHire / NovaCode / NovaVision, Infrastructure, Customer Support, HR, and Marketing.

Champions are the first point of contact for AI use case governance queries, facilitate AUCR submissions, monitor and escalate shadow/unofficial AI use, and attend the quarterly AI Champions forum. Appointed part-time by the business unit head and confirmed by the CRO.

Review Panels & System Owners

Review Panels: Assembled on an as-needed basis to conduct enhanced reviews for high-risk or frontier AI systems. Drawn from Legal/Compliance, the DPO, a CISO delegate, ML Engineering, and external audit/advisory resources.

System Owners: Individual accountability for deployed AI systems. Ensure the system stays within approved scope of use, maintain human oversight procedures, monitor performance, escalate incidents and degradation, manage vendor relationships, and complete an annual System Owner attestation. Appointed at senior-manager level or above and confirmed at AUCR approval.

4. Document 3 — AI Use Case Review & Approval Process (v2.0)

The third document is the operational workflow that governs how every new AI system, frontier model deployment, material change, or significant fine-tune moves from idea to production at NovaAI. It is built on the principle of proportionate governance: lower-risk systems follow a faster path; frontier systems and GPAI with systemic risk implications undergo intensive multi-stage review involving external expertise.

4.1 Process Flow

SUBMISSION
↓
INTAKE TRIAGE — 5 business days
↓
REVIEW & ASSESSMENT — 1 of 4 pathways
↓
APPROVAL DECISION
↓
DEPLOYMENT & REGISTRATION
↓
ONGOING MONITORING

4.2 Stage 1 — Use Case Submission

Submitter: Product owner, research lead, or business owner responsible for the AI system. Mechanism: Via the AI Governance Portal or the AUCR (AI Use Case Review) template.

Required Fields: Business purpose and intended outcomes; intended users and affected individuals; data sources (with personal data identification); model architecture and foundation model used; output type (generative, classification, agentic, etc.); compute scale (for frontier training); human oversight plan; deployment geography; proposed go-live date; system owner nomination; safety evaluation plan.

4.3 Stage 2 — Intake Triage (5 Business Days)

The AI Governance Office performs a completeness check, makes a preliminary EU AI Act classification, conducts a GPAI systemic-risk assessment, and routes the submission to one of four pathways:

Triage OutcomePathwayAction
ProhibitedREJECTNotify CAIO and CRO
Minimal / Limited RiskStandard Review2 weeks
High RiskEnhanced Review4–6 weeks
Frontier / GPAI SystemicFrontier Review8–12 weeks
Classification UnclearLegal + DPO ReviewJoint opinion on classification

4.4 Stage 3A — Standard Review (Minimal / Limited Risk)

Timeline: 2 weeks • Owner: AI Governance Office

Review coverage: Confirmation of risk classification, GDPR lawful basis review, human oversight confirmation, AI disclosure verification (EU AI Act Article 52), and a vendor AI governance check (if a vendor-supplied model is used).

Required documentation: Completed AUCR template, Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA), and a vendor AI governance questionnaire if applicable.

4.5 Stage 3B — Enhanced Review (High Risk)

Timeline: 4–6 weeks • Coordinator: AI Governance Office • Approver: AI Governance Committee

Covers everything in the Standard Review plus a full structured risk assessment, technical documentation review aligned to EU AI Act Annex IV, bias and fairness evaluation, an Article 14 human oversight procedure, an Article 43 conformity assessment plan, an incident response procedure, and a monitoring and performance tracking plan.

Reviewer RoleResponsibility
AI Governance OfficeProcess coordination and documentation review
Data Protection OfficerGDPR compliance and Article 10 requirements
Legal & ComplianceRegulatory interpretation and AI Act compliance
CISO / Information SecurityAdversarial robustness and security risk assessment
ML EngineeringTechnical feasibility and architecture review
External AuditorFairness and bias evaluation (when high-risk with fairness implications)

4.6 Stage 3C — Frontier Review (GPAI Systemic Risk + Novel Capability)

Timeline: 8–12 weeks • Owner: AI Safety Review Board + AI Governance Committee

Covers all Enhanced Review elements plus:

  • Dangerous capability evaluations (CBRN uplift, autonomous replication, cyber, persuasion)
  • Internal red-teaming (minimum 3 weeks)
  • External red-teaming by an independent third party
  • Responsible scaling policy gate check
  • Systemic risk assessment
  • Model card and system card preparation
  • Open-weights release risk analysis (if applicable)
  • EU AI Office notification requirements (Article 52)
  • CEO sign-off gate

Approval authority: AI Safety Review Board (unanimous) + external red team + Chief AI Officer + CEO final approval.

Why this pathway exists: The Frontier Review pathway was formalized in April 2026 following 2025 safety concerns about insufficient governance gates for frontier releases.

4.7 Stage 4 — Approval Decision

PathwayApproval Authority
StandardAI Governance Office + System Owner sign-off
EnhancedAI Governance Committee (majority vote; quorum: CRO + 2 others)
FrontierAI Safety Review Board unanimous + Chief AI Officer + CEO sign-off

Approvals may be conditional, with documented owners and completion dates. Rejections are documented in writing and resubmission is permitted after material changes. Escalation: Enhanced → AI Governance Committee → CRO → Board. Frontier → AI Safety Review Board → CAIO → CEO → Board AI Safety Sub-Committee.

4.8 Stage 5 — Deployment & Registration

Pre-go-live requirements: Register the system in the AI System Inventory; complete all approval conditions; confirm monitoring systems are operational; and confirm human oversight procedures are in place and trained.

Regulatory registration: EU AI Act Article 71 database registration for high-risk systems; EU AI Office notification for GPAI with systemic risk. The AI Governance Office issues final go-live sign-off.

Out-of-cycle review triggers: material change to scope, purpose, or data sources; an AI incident or adverse impact report; regulatory change affecting the system; system owner change; new capability discovered; performance drift or model degradation.

4.10 Material Changes, Fast-Track & Emergency

Material changes trigger a new AUCR or change request. They include: scope/purpose/objective change; new personal data category or data source; significant model update (fine-tune, retraining, architecture change); foundation model version upgrade; new vendor or third-party provider; new deployment geography; system owner change; compute scale upgrade (for frontier systems); and changes in human oversight requirements.

Security patches and bug fixes are documented without requiring new review cycles — the AI Governance Office retains discretion to require expedited review if a significant behaviour change occurs.

Emergency System Deprecation: The Chief AI Officer may unilaterally suspend any system in case of identified imminent harm. The AI Governance Committee is notified within 24 hours; formal review of the suspension occurs within 5 business days.
