1. Executive Summary

TradeFlow Inc. is a US-headquartered, self-clearing broker-dealer that provides brokerage infrastructure via institutional-grade APIs. Founded in 2018 in Chicago, TradeFlow enables fintech companies, investment advisors, wealth managers, hedge funds, and neo-banks to embed trading functionality into their own applications. The company serves ~200 institutional clients powering ~6 million retail brokerage accounts across 35 countries, and is regulated by the SEC, FINRA, MiFID II (Dublin), and FCA (London).

In January 2026, TradeFlow launched TradeFlow Insight — a transformer-based AI recommendation engine providing personalised stock and ETF suggestions to 1.8 million retail accounts across 62 institutional clients. This project delivers a comprehensive 6-document cyber and AI risk programme covering the company profile, cyber risk assessment, AI risk assessment, vendor risk assessment, regulatory landscape mapping, and AI governance policy.

3. Cyber Risk Assessment

This assessment identifies 12 cybersecurity risks scored on a 5×5 Likelihood–Impact matrix, aligned to the NIST Cybersecurity Framework and mapped to ISO 27001 Annex A controls. Each risk is scored for inherent (before controls) and residual (after controls) risk. TradeFlow's risk appetite: no residual risk at High or Critical level.

Scoring Framework

Score RangeLevelTreatment
1–6LowAccept with monitoring
7–14MediumReduce with controls, review quarterly
15–19HighReduce to Medium within defined timeline
20–25CriticalImmediate action, executive escalation

12-Risk Cyber Register

C01 — Unauthorised Access to Trading API

Attackers compromise API credentials (OAuth tokens or API keys) to execute unauthorised trades or exfiltrate account data. Highest-impact risk — API directly handles financial transactions.

NIST CSF: PR.AC, DE.CM  |  ISO 27001: A.9.1, A.9.4

Inherent: 20 — Critical → Residual: 10 — Medium

Controls: OAuth 2.0 with 15-min token expiry, API key rotation every 90 days, rate limiting, IP allowlisting for enterprise clients, real-time anomaly detection on trading patterns, 24/7 SOC monitoring.

C02 — Cloud Infrastructure Misconfiguration (GCP)

Misconfigured GCP resources (open storage buckets, permissive firewall rules, exposed Kubernetes endpoints) create exploitable attack surface.

NIST CSF: ID.AM, PR.AC  |  ISO 27001: A.12.6, A.13.1

Inherent: 16 — High → Residual: 8 — Medium

Controls: GCP Security Command Center, IaC (Terraform) with peer review, CIS Benchmark enforcement, automated drift detection, monthly audits.

C03 — Insider Threat — Privileged Access Misuse

Employee or contractor with elevated privileges abuses access to steal data, manipulate trades, or tamper with the AI model.

Inherent: 15 — High → Residual: 8 — Medium

Controls: Least-privilege RBAC, JIT access, all privileged actions logged and alerted, quarterly access reviews, background checks, separation of duties.

C04 — DDoS Attack on API Gateway

DDoS overwhelms the API gateway, preventing trade execution during market hours — a regulatory and financial risk.

Inherent: 16 — High → Residual: 6 — Low

Controls: Cloud Armor adaptive DDoS, multi-region failover, auto-scaling, rate limiting, quarterly DDoS simulation exercises.

C05 — Data Breach — Client PII and Financial Data

Exfiltration of PII and financial data of 6 million accounts across 35 countries. Severe regulatory exposure under GDPR, FINRA, and SEC.

Inherent: 15 — High → Residual: 8 — Medium

Controls: AES-256 at rest, TLS 1.3 in transit, CMEK, DLP rules, PII masking in logs, annual penetration testing, breach response plan tested semi-annually.

C06 — Supply Chain Attack via Software Dependencies

Compromised third-party library or container image introduces malicious code. Lessons from SolarWinds and Log4Shell.

Inherent: 12 — Medium → Residual: 8 — Medium

Controls: SBOM for all services, automated scanning (Snyk), signed container images, pinned dependency versions, monthly vulnerability review.

C07 — Inadequate Logging and Audit Trail

Insufficient logging prevents event reconstruction during incidents and fails regulatory examination requirements (FINRA 17a-4 requires 7-year retention).

Inherent: 12 — Medium → Residual: 4 — Low

Controls: Centralised logging (Cloud Logging), SIEM integration (Chronicle), immutable 7-year retention, API and model inference logging, quarterly completeness audits.

C08 — Phishing and Social Engineering

Targeted phishing against engineering and finance teams to obtain internal system credentials.

Inherent: 12 — Medium → Residual: 6 — Low

Controls: MFA on all accounts (hardware keys for privileged), quarterly phishing simulations, email filtering and sandboxing.

C09 — Ransomware Attack

Ransomware encrypts production systems, disrupting trading operations. Double extortion risk with data exfiltration.

Inherent: 15 — High → Residual: 8 — Medium

Controls: Cross-region backups with integrity testing, network segmentation, EDR, ransomware-specific IR playbook, cyber insurance.

C10 — Third-Party Vendor Breach (MarketBridge)

MarketBridge compromised, injecting manipulated market data or exposing TradeFlow data. See Document 4 for full vendor assessment.

Inherent: 12 — Medium → Residual: 8 — Medium

Controls: Annual vendor assessment, SOC 2 review, contractual right to audit, data validation on incoming data, MarketBridge has no access to user data.

C11 — Weak Vulnerability Management

Unpatched vulnerabilities in OS, libraries, or applications create exploitable entry points.

Inherent: 12 — Medium → Residual: 6 — Low

Controls: Weekly scanning, Critical/High CVEs patched within 7/30 days, GKE auto-upgrade, bi-annual external pen test.

C12 — Business Continuity & Disaster Recovery Failure

Major outage exceeds recovery capabilities, resulting in extended trading downtime with regulatory consequences.

Inherent: 10 — Medium → Residual: 5 — Low

Controls: Multi-region GCP, automated failover, RTO 1 hour / RPO 15 minutes, quarterly DR testing, FINRA BCP filed.

4. AI Risk Assessment — TradeFlow Insight

This assessment identifies 10 AI-specific risks for TradeFlow Insight, aligned to the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) and the EU AI Act. TradeFlow Insight qualifies as a potential high-risk AI system under the Act because it influences financial decisions for individual users (Article 6 and Annex III). Risk appetite: no residual risk should exceed Medium level.

10-Risk AI Register

AI01 — Model Bias and Unfair Recommendations

Training data and model architecture systematically favour large-cap US stocks and established ETFs, disadvantaging non-US investors and those seeking diversification. Bias originates from MarketBridge data composition, US market dominance in training data, and correlation between portfolio size and access to diverse securities.

NIST AI RMF: Map, Measure, Manage  |  EU AI Act: Art. 10, 15

Inherent: 16 — High → Residual: 6 — Low

Controls: Monthly bias audit with demographic/geographic stratification, fairness metrics (disparate impact, equalized odds), explicit training on international securities, quarterly CIO review, complaints dashboard.

AI02 — Inadequate Explainability of Recommendations

Transformer architecture is inherently opaque. Users cannot understand why the model selected a particular stock or ETF. Regulatory risk under SEC Regulation SHO, FINRA Rule 4512, and EU AI Act Article 13.

NIST AI RMF: Map, Measure, Manage  |  EU AI Act: Art. 13, 14

Inherent: 16 — High → Residual: 6 — Low

Controls: SHAP values for each recommendation, top 5 contributing factors surfaced to user, explainability report attached, quarterly user feedback surveys, compliance review of disclosure language.

AI03 — Regulatory Classification Ambiguity

Highest-severity risk (25/25 inherent): Is TradeFlow Insight “investment advice” under SEC Rule 206(4)-1 and FINRA Rule 4512, or merely “information”? If classified as advice, TradeFlow must register as an investment adviser, comply with fiduciary duty, maintain E&O insurance, and conduct suitability analysis. Misclassification exposes the firm to enforcement action, civil litigation, and forced operational changes.

NIST AI RMF: Govern  |  EU AI Act: Art. 6, 8

Inherent: 25 — Critical → Residual: 10 — Medium

Controls: Formal legal opinion from external SEC/FINRA counsel (Q2 2026), written disclosure to all institutional clients, pre-approval of all user-facing messaging by legal, quarterly reassessment, proactive SEC/FINRA no-action letter if warranted.

AI04 — Data Governance Risks

Trained on 24 months of trade data, portfolios, risk profiles, and real-time market data. Risks include: insufficient user consent for AI training, over-retention, cross-border transfers without GDPR adequacy/SCCs, and de-identification shortcomings.

Inherent: 16 — High → Residual: 8 — Medium

Controls: Updated consent form disclosing AI usage, 24-month retention justified, SCCs for EU model training, annual DPIA, data minimisation review, quarterly DPO review.

AI05 — Model Drift and Performance Degradation

Market conditions shift rapidly (bull to bear, sector rotations). Model trained during 2024–25 may underperform during 2026 regime changes without detection.

Inherent: 16 — High → Residual: 6 — Low

Controls: Weekly performance dashboard (adoption rate, 30-day return vs. benchmark), automated alerts if adoption drops >10% WoW, monthly backtesting, threshold-based retraining (Sharpe ratio <0.5), documented rollback runbook.

AI06 — Adversarial Manipulation and Gaming

Users could submit crafted portfolio inputs for pump-and-dump schemes, portfolio spoofing, or model extraction attacks to reverse-engineer decision boundaries.

Inherent: 12 — Medium → Residual: 6 — Low

Controls: Input validation (reject implausible portfolios), rate limiting (50 calls/day), anomaly detection, correlation checks across user IDs, FINRA surveillance integration.

AI07 — Hallucination and Invalid Output

Model could recommend delisted stocks, non-existent tickers, or OFAC-sanctioned companies. Damages credibility and creates compliance violations.

Inherent: 15 — High → Residual: 5 — Low

Controls: Post-processing validation against live MarketBridge security master, OFAC/EU sanctions check, price staleness check (<5 min), automated filtering with substitute candidates, weekly rejected recommendation audit.

AI08 — Privacy Violation and Training Data Leakage

Transformer model trained on millions of user records could memorise individual trading patterns. Membership inference, model inversion, and extraction attacks could expose sensitive financial information.

Inherent: 15 — High → Residual: 4 — Low

Controls: Differential privacy (epsilon=2.0), aggregated statistics over individual records, extraction attack detection, annual external privacy assessment, GDPR consent framework, 30-day log retention limit.

AI09 — Prompt Injection via API

Malformed API requests with embedded instructions could trick the system into leaking training data, bypassing validation, or manipulating behaviour.

Inherent: 9 — Medium → Residual: 6 — Low

Controls: Strict JSON schema validation, API sandboxing (isolated container), rate limiting, OAuth 2.0 scoped tokens, quarterly pen testing of Insight API, security headers (CSP, X-Frame-Options).

AI10 — Lack of Human Oversight and Intervention

Recommendations flow model-to-user with no human-in-the-loop review. Harmful trades (penny stock concentration, illiquid securities) could reach users without intervention.

Inherent: 16 — High → Residual: 6 — Low

Controls: “Review Required” mode for institutional clients, CCO override for user cohorts, daily compliance anomaly report, escalation runbook, quarterly case reviews of complaints and model errors.

AI-Specific Vendor Risks

Data Integrity — Manipulated Market Data

If MarketBridge feeds are compromised, injected market signals could poison Insight model outputs, causing poor recommendations. Mitigation: Anomaly detection on inbound data, cross-validation with alternative sources, circuit breakers that halt the AI model if data quality degrades.

Data Freshness — Stale Market Data

Delayed or incomplete data causes stale recommendations. Mitigation: SLA specifies <50ms latency, real-time monitoring with alerts at >100ms, model rejects data older than 5 minutes.

Vendor Lock-In — Single Source Dependency

MarketBridge is the sole market data provider. Mitigation: 180-day termination notice, API abstraction layer for provider swapping, evaluation of Bloomberg/Refinitiv for redundancy.

6. Regulatory Landscape & Compliance Mapping

TradeFlow operates across three jurisdictions as a broker-dealer, sitting at the intersection of financial services regulation, cybersecurity regulation, and emerging AI regulation. This document maps all applicable regulatory obligations.

US Regulatory Landscape

SEC & FINRA — Broker-Dealer Obligations

  • Broker-Dealer Registration (Section 15, Securities Exchange Act) — ongoing fitness, capital, and audit requirements
  • Regulation SCI (17 CFR Part 242) — system compliance and integrity, incident notification, annual vulnerability assessments
  • Trade Surveillance (FINRA Rule 6730) — monitoring for manipulation. If Insight concentrates trading in specific securities, surveillance must detect patterns.
  • Books & Records (SEC Rule 17a-4, FINRA Rule 4530) — 6-year retention of all trading activity, AI recommendation logs, compliance overrides
  • Customer Protection (SEC Rule 15c3-3) — asset segregation and net capital requirements
  • Anti-Fraud (SEC Rule 10b-5, FINRA 5210–5290) — all communications including AI-generated recommendations must not be deceptive
Critical Regulatory Risk — AI as “Investment Advice”: The SEC has clarified (2024–2025) that AI-generated stock recommendations may constitute “investment advice” if they are personalised and go beyond generic information. If classified as advice, TradeFlow must register as an investment adviser and comply with fiduciary standards. TradeFlow positions Insight as an “informational tool” with disclaimers, but this is subject to regulatory challenge — scored as Critical risk (AI03).

NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

Effectively mandatory for financial institutions due to SEC guidance, insurance requirements, and OCC/Federal Reserve references. TradeFlow has achieved substantial alignment. No major gaps identified.

State-Level AI Regulation

  • Colorado AI Act (2024): Requires high-risk AI impact assessments — Insight may qualify
  • NYC Local Law 144: AI bias audits in hiring — signals regulatory direction toward algorithmic transparency
  • SEC Proposed Rules (2024–2025): Conflicts of interest in AI, predictive analytics suitability, algorithm governance

EU Regulatory Landscape

EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689)

TradeFlow Insight is likely NOT classified as high-risk under Annex III as currently written (stock recommendations are not explicitly listed). However, TradeFlow treats Insight AS IF high-risk, implementing Articles 6, 8, 37, and 50 requirements. The definition is evolving and could be expanded via delegated acts.

  • Article 6: Conformity assessment including risk assessment, testing, and quality management
  • Article 8: Technical documentation of AI design, training data, validation, and limitations
  • Article 37: Post-market monitoring for bias, drift, and recommendation quality
  • Article 50: Transparency — users must know they interact with AI and understand how recommendations work

GDPR

Applies because EU-based clients and their investors are identifiable natural persons. Key tensions: Article 6 lawful basis (contract), Article 22 automated decision-making (TradeFlow argues recommendations are informational, not decisions), and Article 25 data minimisation for AI training.

MiFID II & DORA

  • MiFID II: Investment services directive. If Insight is “advice,” TradeFlow needs MiFID II “investment advice” authorisation from ESMA — parallel to the SEC classification question.
  • DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act): Maps critical third-party dependencies, incident logging/reporting, penetration testing, vendor exit plans. Insight is critical to service — must be tested under stress and have a failure plan. MarketBridge vendor assessment supports DORA compliance.

UK Regulatory Landscape

FCA & UK GDPR

FCA authorisation for the London subsidiary. Consumer Duty (2023) imposes “good outcomes” obligation for AI recommendations. UK GDPR mirrors EU GDPR with UK-specific adequacy decisions. FCA AI and Machine Learning guidance requires fairness, explainability, and governance documentation.

7. AI Governance Policy & Controls

This policy establishes the governance framework, ownership structure, and monitoring programme for all AI systems deployed within TradeFlow. It ensures accountability, transparency, fairness, continuous monitoring, and regulatory compliance.

Six Governance Principles

PrincipleDescription
SafetyAI systems must not cause financial harm. Recommendations are never automatically executed. Failures must be detected and contained immediately.
FairnessNo systematic bias disadvantaging any user segment by account size, geography, trading style, or demographics.
TransparencyUsers must always know they interact with AI, understand how it works, and know its limitations.
AccountabilityEvery AI decision and failure has a named owner. Ownership concentrated in specific roles (Model Owner, Risk Owner).
PrivacyAI systems minimise personal data usage. Data minimisation principles embedded in model development.
ComplianceAll AI systems meet or exceed all applicable regulatory requirements across all jurisdictions.

Governance Structure

AI Governance Committee

Members: CISO (Chair), CCO (Vice Chair), CTO, Head of Data Science, DPO, General Counsel

Cadence: Monthly standing meetings (90 min minimum). Emergency meetings within 24 hours for critical AI incidents.

Responsibilities: Review and approve all new AI models before production. Quarterly review of performance and fairness metrics. Oversee AI incident response. Review regulatory updates. Approve material model changes. Receive briefings on emerging AI threats (prompt injection, adversarial attacks).

AI Model Owner — Head of Data Science

Accountable for end-to-end lifecycle of all production AI models: performance metrics (accuracy, latency, precision, recall), fairness and bias monitoring, monthly retraining approval, root cause analysis for degradation, and AI kill switch execution. Can escalate directly to CISO or CEO/Board.

AI Risk Owner — Cyber GRC Lead

Maintains AI Risk Register, conducts quarterly control testing (fairness metrics, kill switch functionality), generates audit evidence for external auditors (SOC 2, ISO 27001), coordinates with DPO on data protection, and interfaces with external auditors on AI governance scope.

Human Oversight Controls

  • Mandatory Disclaimer: All clients contractually required to display: “TradeFlow Insight recommendations are informational only and do not constitute investment advice.”
  • No Auto-Execution: Insight cannot execute trades. Hardcoded — no trade execution endpoints available from Insight API.
  • Global Kill Switch: Head of Data Science or CISO can disable Insight globally within 15 minutes. Automatic client notification within 1 hour.
  • Client-Level Disable: Any client can turn off Insight instantly via API or UI. No approval required.
  • Quarterly Outcome Review: 500+ recommendation sample reviewed for outcomes, false positive rates, and underperforming cohorts.

What Constitutes an AI Incident

  • Widespread identical recommendations (>30% of users receiving same stock) — herding risk
  • Fairness metric breach (>10% gap between user segments)
  • Regulatory query or examination request about recommendations
  • Accuracy drops below 55% within a 7-day window
  • Drift score exceeds critical threshold
